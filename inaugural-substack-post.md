# Why Strategy Matters More Than the Meta

*Inaugural post - Wargames Institute*

---

I didn't start wargaming to chase tournament wins or find the optimal army list.

I came to the hobby with a specific curiosity: **how do tabletop games model strategic decision-making?**

My background is in strategic theory—I studied it during my service in the United States Marine Corps, where concepts like centers of gravity, culminating points, and ends-ways-means aren't academic exercises but practical frameworks for thinking through complex problems.

When I discovered wargaming, I realized these games do something remarkable: they turn abstract strategic concepts into playable systems you can experiment with, fail at, learn from, and improve through—all without real-world consequences.

## What I'm Building Here

Wargames Institute exists to explore strategic thinking through wargames.

The wargaming community is incredible. There's amazing content out there—tournament coverage, painting tutorials, rules discussions, list optimization, narrative battle reports. All valuable. All part of what makes this hobby great.

**What I want to add to that conversation is a strategic lens.**

Most content (understandably) focuses on tactics: how to win fights, position units, use terrain, build competitive lists. That's important and useful.

But there's another layer worth exploring:

- What strategic principles do these mechanics actually model?
- How do different rule systems create different decision spaces?
- What can we learn about strategy itself by playing these games?
- How do concepts from military doctrine manifest on the tabletop?

## What I Mean by "Strategy"

In military doctrine, **strategy** and **tactics** are distinct:

**Tactics** is about winning individual engagements—how to fight.

**Strategy** is about achieving objectives through available resources—what to fight for and when.

Most "strategy guides" in wargaming are actually tactics guides. They tell you how to win a fight. That's valuable.

Strategy asks different questions:
- What am I actually trying to accomplish?
- How do I get there given my constraints?
- What can I afford to lose? What must I protect?
- When should I attack? When should I consolidate?
- Where is the decisive point?

These aren't game-specific questions. They apply to Warmachine, Infinity, BattleTech, Next War, Littoral Commander, BLKOUT, Kill Team, and every other wargame you'll play.

## Why This Matters for You

Whether you're playing a 30-minute skirmish or a 6-hour operational monster, understanding strategic thinking changes how you see the game.

Not because you'll necessarily win more (though you might).

But because you'll **understand what's happening** on the table.

You'll recognize patterns across different games. You'll see why certain mechanics work the way they do. You'll make better decisions under pressure. You'll appreciate the design choices more deeply.

Most importantly: **you'll develop your own strategic thinking** instead of just following someone else's approach.

## The First Exercise

Before we dive into formal strategy in the next post, here's your first exercise:

**Think about your last game.** Not the one you won easily or lost badly—pick a close one where decisions mattered.

Now ask yourself:
- What was I *actually* trying to accomplish? (Not "win"—what were the specific objectives?)
- Did I have a plan for how to achieve that?
- What resources did I commit? What did I hold back?
- At what point did my plan change? Why?
- If I played it again, what would I do differently?

Don't overthink it. Just reflect honestly.
