# From the Marine Corps to the Tabletop: Why Strategy Matters More Than the Meta

*Inaugural post - Wargames Institute*

---

I didn't start wargaming to chase tournament wins or find the optimal army list.

I came to the hobby after spending years in the United States Marine Corps, where I studied strategic theory not as an academic exercise, but as a practical necessity. When you're responsible for Marines' lives and mission success, you learn quickly that strategy isn't just a buzzword—it's the difference between achieving your objectives and failing catastrophically.

After leaving active service, I found myself drawn to wargames. Not the painting (though I respect the craft), not the social aspect (though the community is great), but the *systems*. These games model decisions, friction, and strategic trade-offs in ways that let you experiment, fail, learn, and improve without real-world consequences.

But here's what struck me: **most wargaming content misses the point entirely.**

## The Problem with Meta-Chasing

Open any wargaming forum, YouTube channel, or Facebook group, and you'll find the same thing:

- "Top 10 armies for the new meta"
- "This list is broken"
- "How to counter [flavor-of-the-month build]"
- Tournament reports that are just glorified dice rolls

There's nothing *wrong* with competitive play. But when that's **all** the content available, we lose something important.

We lose the opportunity to think strategically.

## What I Mean by "Strategy"

In the Marine Corps, we define strategy through MCDP-1 "Warfighting" and related doctrine. It's not complicated:

**Strategy** is about achieving your ends (objectives) through ways (methods) with available means (resources).

**Tactics** is about winning individual engagements.

Most "strategy guides" in wargaming are actually tactics guides. They tell you how to win a fight, how to position units, how to use terrain. That's valuable. But it's not strategy.

Strategy asks different questions:
- What am I actually trying to accomplish?
- How do I get there given my constraints?
- What can I afford to lose? What can't I lose?
- When should I attack? When should I consolidate?
- Where is the enemy vulnerable? Where am I vulnerable?

These aren't game-specific questions. They apply to Warmachine, Infinity, BattleTech, Next War, Littoral Commander, Blackout, and every other wargame you'll play.

## Why This Matters for You

Whether you're playing a 30-minute skirmish or a 6-hour operational monster, understanding strategic thinking makes you better.

Not because you'll win more (though you might).

But because you'll *understand* what's happening on the table.

You'll stop making the same mistakes repeatedly. You'll recognize patterns across different games. You'll see why certain mechanics work the way they do. You'll make better decisions under pressure.

Most importantly: **you'll think for yourself instead of copying someone else's list.**

I'm not here to tell you what army to play. I'm not going to give you optimized lists or tournament-winning combinations. Other people do that better than I ever could, and that's fine.

## A Note on "Former Marines"

You might notice I said I "served in" the United States Marine Corps, not that I'm a "former Marine."

There's a saying: "Once a Marine, always a Marine."

It's not about rank or service status. It's about how the experience shapes how you think. The Marine Corps taught me to think systematically, to plan deliberately, to analyze honestly, and to always ask "what's the mission?"

Those habits don't leave you when you leave service. And they're exactly what wargaming needs more of.

## The First Lesson Starts Now

Before we dive into formal strategy in the next post, here's your first exercise:

**Think about your last game.** Not the one you won easily or lost badly—pick a close one where decisions mattered.

Now ask yourself:
- What was I *actually* trying to accomplish? (Not "win"—what were the specific objectives?)
- Did I have a plan for how to achieve that?
- What resources did I commit? What did I hold back?
- At what point did my plan change? Why?
- If I played it again, what would I do differently?

Don't overthink it. Just reflect honestly.
